Part II: Dynamics (The Science of Motion)

The Core of Dynamics: The moment the forces on a body are unbalanced, it will accelerate. Dynamics is split into two halves: Kinematics (the geometry of motion like speed and distance, ignoring the push) and Kinetics (connecting the push to the motion using F=ma and Energy principles).

Deep Dive: The Dynamics Syllabus

THE BASICS

1.0 & 2.0 Kinematics of Particle

Tracking motion without worrying about the forces causing it. This covers straight lines, curves, and projectiles.

  • 1.1-1.6 Newton's Laws, Gravitation, Formulation of Dynamic Problems
  • 2.1-2.2 Rectilinear Motion (Straight line)
  • 2.3 Plane Curvilinear Motion (Curves & Projectiles)
O'yemi's Analogy: Think of Kinematics as the speedometer on a dashboard. It tells you how fast you are going and how far you've traveled (using v = u + at, and s = ut + ½at²), but it doesn't care how big the car's engine is.
NEWTON IN ACTION

3.0 Kinetics of Particles

This is where we bring the forces into the equation. It's the literal application of Newton's Second Law.

  • 3.1-3.2 Force, Mass and Acceleration (F = ma)

O'yemi's Note: Always remember that Acceleration 'a' is a vector. If a car is braking, 'a' is negative! Don't mess up your signs when setting up your F = ma equations.
SURE EXAM QUESTION

4.0 Work and Energy

Solving complex motion problems using the Conservation of Energy instead of calculating forces directly.

  • 4.1 Work
  • 4.2 Energy (Kinetic and Potential)
  • 4.3 Power
O'yemi's Exam Hack: Remember the "Body projected up an inclined plane" PQ! You must know that Kinetic Energy = ½mv², and that the Work Done against friction is Frictional Force × Distance. Energy Initial - Work Lost = Energy Final!
SURE EXAM QUESTION

5.0 Impulse and Momentum

What happens during a crash? This section deals with collisions and explosions.

  • 5.1 Some Definitions (Linear Momentum)
  • 5.2 Angular Momentum
O'yemi's Exam Hack: The "2 kg and 4 kg ball impact" PQ appears almost every year. Memorize the Conservation of Momentum formula: m₁u₁ + m₂u₂ = m₁v₁ + m₂v₂. And don't forget the Coefficient of Restitution (e) = (v₂ - v₁) / (u₁ - u₂)!
DRAWING REQUIRED

6.0 Velocity in Simple Mechanisms

Analyzing the moving parts of machines, like the crank and connecting rod of a car engine.

  • 6.1 Relative Velocity in Mechanisms

  • 6.3 Procedure for Velocity Diagram
O'yemi's Note: You will likely get a "four-bar chain" or "engine mechanism" question. You must know how to calculate angular velocity (ω = 2πN/60) and how to draw a space diagram and a velocity vector polygon to find relative sliding velocities. Grab your rulers and protractors!
