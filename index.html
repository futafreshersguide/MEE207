Course Roadmap: What Are We Studying?

Part I: Statics (The Art of Staying Still)

Statics is all about bodies at rest or moving at a constant velocity. The forces are fighting, but the net result is zero.

  • 1.0 Introduction: Concepts, Principles, and Free Body Diagrams
  • 2.0 Vector Representation: Forces, Moments, Couples, and Equilibrium
  • 3.0 Virtual Work: Work of a force, Potential Energy, and Stability
  • 4.0 Centroid & Centers of Gravity: 2D bodies, Integration techniques
  • 5.0 Simple Structures: Trusses, Frames, and Machines

Part II: Dynamics (The Science of Motion)

Dynamics is where things get moving. It's the study of bodies in motion and the forces that cause that motion.

  • 1.0 Introduction: Newton's Laws and Gravitation
  • 2.0 Kinematics of Particle: Rectilinear and Plane Curvilinear Motion
  • 3.0 Kinetics of Particles: Force, Mass, Acceleration, and Equations of Motion
  • 4.0 Work and Energy: Work, Energy, and Power
  • 5.0 Impulse and Momentum: Linear and Angular Momentum
  • 6.0 Velocity in Simple Mechanisms: Relative velocity and Sliding Blocks

Mental Models & Analogies

💡 The "Tug-of-War" Analogy for Statics:
Imagine a massive game of tug-of-war where both sides are pulling with exactly 5,000 Newtons of force. The rope isn't moving, but it is under immense tension. That is Statics. The forces are actively fighting, but the net result is zero.
💡 The "Rollercoaster" Analogy for Dynamics:
When a rollercoaster sits at the very top of a drop, it has high Potential Energy and zero Kinetic Energy. When it drops, gravity accelerates it, and the tracks exert a normal force to keep it from flying away. Tracking those exact changes second-by-second is Dynamics.

General Expectations & Survival Tips

CRITICAL HINT

1. The FBD is Your Best Friend

You cannot solve a mechanics problem if you cannot see the forces. Before you write down a single equation, isolate the object and draw arrows for every single force acting on it. An FBD is worth 50% of the solution.

NOTE

2. Master Your Units

Answers without units are just random numbers. Always ensure you are working in consistent SI units. If you multiply mass (kg) by acceleration (m/s2), you get kg·m/s2, which is a Newton (N).

NOTE

3. Respect the Math

Applied mechanics relies heavily on trigonometry (SOH CAH TOA is your daily bread), resolving vectors into X and Y components, and basic calculus for Centroids and Kinematics. Brush up on these immediately.

PRO TIP

4. Practice Over Reading

Reading a textbook on mechanics is like reading a textbook on how to ride a bicycle. You won't learn until you actually try it. You must solve problems with a pen, paper, and calculator. See Page 4 for past questions!
